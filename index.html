Hot Air Balloon Sim (Teacher's Beta)

Easiest way to get a feel for what's going on, pre-art assets and pre-instructinoal prompts.

1. Refresh the page (start from a new, untouched game)
2. Press and hold the top-left-most green button
- Notice the temperature rising in the balloon.
- The blue line (currently pointing down) will eventually shrink and invert, pointing up.
- - this represents the balloon's acceleration as a function of its size and temperature compared to that of the outside world.
3. The balloon will eventually rise slightly, then stop
4. You can press the bottom green button to "cut the invisible ropes" holding the balloon down before takeoff
- The purpose of these ropes is to just give you time to adjust your accelleration however you'd like before you accidentally launch into space
5. You can use the middle button to "release a bit of air" from the balloon, effectively lowering the temp of the balloon
6. See how long you can go without hitting the ground or launching into space!

Some notes about the sim:
This simulation is reasonably physically accurate, taking into account changes in density due to temperature, and that compared to the volume of the balloon, etc...
All behind-the-scenes units (volume of balloon, weight of unfilled balloon, outside air dew pt, etc...) are reasonably accurate
One aspect of the sim not yet in place is that of diminishing atmospheric pressure as one gets higher (hence, the unhindered ability to launch into space)

Are the concepts explored age/complexity appropriate?
Is there some interesting aspect of bouyancy/convection we're missing?
Are we wasting time concerning ourselves with some aspect of bouyancy/convection that isn't interesting?

Some prompts regarding those previous questions:
There is complex physics around differences in density resulting in bouyancy in the presence of gravity- this game doesn't really get at any of that.
It seems instead to just drive home "hot air goes up", without really exploring why.
Is that ok? Is this the stage at which we want to be engaging the students?
- If so, how can we make this more interesting? Create obstacles to avoid?
- - At this level, the physical correctness of the simulation seems less important- should we scrap that in favor of a more intuitive control scheme?
- - Does "indirectly" controlling the balloon add to the understanding? (add heat, which changes temp, which changes acceleration, which changes velocity, which changes position)
- - Or would it be better served "adding heat directly changes velocity" or even "position"?
- If not, what can we do to bring in the more complex concepts of how and why bouyancy works? what interesting visualizations, mechanics, interactions, etc... could be used?
